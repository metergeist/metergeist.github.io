History

When Paul Franke and Reinhold Heidecke introduced the Rolleiflex in 1929, they created more than a camera — they defined a category. The original used 117 film for 6x6cm exposures and established the twin-lens reflex design that would dominate professional photography for decades.

Notable Photographers

Established the TLR as a serious tool for professional photographers.

Cultural Significance

Recognized as one of the most influential camera designs in history.

Collector Notes

The camera that started it all. Uses obsolete 117 film. Primarily of collector interest.

Buying Tips

The original Rolleiflex is a pre-war collectible first and a usable camera second. Here is what to check before buying.

  • Bellows condition: Unlike later rigid-body Rolleiflexes, the Original uses bellows for focusing. Inspect carefully for pinholes and light leaks by shining a light inside the camera in a dark room.
  • Focusing knob: The focusing knob can become stiff or seized with age. Turn it through the full range and check for smooth, even movement.
  • Film advance: The film advance mechanism can skip or jam. Cycle it several times to confirm reliable operation.
  • Red window counter: This camera uses a red window film counter rather than an automatic mechanism. Confirm the red window is intact and not faded beyond usefulness.
  • Display vs functional: Many units on the market are decorative or display-only pieces. Always confirm the camera is mechanically functional before paying working-camera prices.
  • Lens coating: Lens coating varies by production era. Earlier uncoated lenses are more prone to flare. Check for haze, fungus, and separation in both taking and viewing lenses.
